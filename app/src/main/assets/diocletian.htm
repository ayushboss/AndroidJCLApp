Diocletian (284-305 AD)

 

1.                  1)   Diocletian came from a provincial Danubian family. The sources, probably inaccurately, claim he had very humble origins.

a. a.    Diocletian had been a cavalryman under Gallienus, a cavalry commander in Moesia, and a commandant of the Imperial mounted bodyguard.

b.                  2)   Diocletian chose Maximian, an old comrade-in-arms, to guard the West while he saw to the East. He elevated Maximian to the rank of Caesar and, after Maximian crushed the Bacaudae (a group of desperate, rebelling peasants) in Gaul, Diocletian made Maximian a co-Augustus.

c. 3)   Maximian established the naval port of Gesoriacum (Bononia, Boulogne) in Gaul to combat Frankish and Saxon pirates raiding the shores of Gaul and Britain.

d.                  a.    Maximian sent Marcus Aurelius Carausius to take command of Gesoriacum. Carausius defeated the pirates, but then seized Gesoriacum and Britain for himself and conferred the title Augustus on himself. For a while, Carausius ruled as the “Emperor of the North”, maintaining hold over Britain for seven years.

e. 4)   In the East, Diocletian successfully negotiated with the Persian king Bahram, making Persia renounce its claim to Mesopotamia, and recognize Tiridates “III” as Roman client king of Armenia.

f.   5)   Diocletian forms the Tetrarchy

g. a.    Diocletian and Maximian, both augusti, each chose a subordinate caesar.

h.                  b.    In the East, Diocletian chose Galerius as his Caesar.

i.   c.    In the West, Maximian chose Constantius Chlorus (“Pale-faced”) as his Caesar. Constantius Chlorus (and his son, Constantine) were from Naissus.

j.    d.    Each Caesar was the adopted successor and son-in-law of his Augustus.

k.                  6)   Constantius Chlorus captured Gesoriacum from Carausius. In 293, a rival treacherously assassinated Carausius, allowing Constantius Chlorus to re-establish Roman Rule in Britain

l.   7)   Meanwhile, Narses overthrew Bahram II to become king of the Persians. Narses drove Tiridates “III” out of Armenia.

m.               a.    Galerius captured Narses’ harem, regained control of Mesopotamia, and seized the fortress of Nisibis and the Persian capital of Ctesiphon.

n.                  b.    After subduing Egypt, Diocletian joined Galerius at Nisibis and made peace with Narses, under which Narses surrendered Mesopotamia, acknowledged Armenia, etc.

o.                  8)   In Egypt, Domitius Domitianus and Aurelius Achilleus revolted under Diocletian. Diocletian brought an army from Syria and besieged Alexandria; eventually, he subdued all of Egypt.

p.                  9)   Diocletian’s Reforms

q.                  a.    Diocletian assumed the title of Jovius, indicating he was Jupiter’s earthly representative sent to restore the Roman Empire.

i.                 i.     Maximian took the title of Herculius.

r.  b.    Diocletian adopted an elaborate court ceremonial, dressing in purple and gold and jewels, wearing the diadem (a royal headdress) and carrying the scepter. People had to kneel and kiss his robe.

s.  c.    Dicoletian divided Italy into a dozen provinces. He subdivided all the old provinces and grouped them into twelve administrative districts known as dioceses. Each diocese was subject to a vicar (vicarius).

t.   d.    Comitatus: personal escort of a Caesar or Augustus. Fabricae: state-owned military workshops.

u.                  e.    Diocletian issued the Edict on Maximum Prices in 301 which set ceilings on the prices of a thousand different items.

i.                 i.     Diocletian also reformed the coinax and instituted tax reforms.

v. f.     Diocletian broke the forty-year religious truce proclaimed by Gallienus and started persecuting Christians again at the end of his career.

i.                 i.     Galerius was especially zealous to persecute the Christians, while Constantius Chlorus didn’t really do much.

ii.             ii.   Incident where Diocletian orders all palace members to sacrifice to the gods, after augurs say “hostile influences” have hindered them.

iii.          iii.  Diocletian’s own wife was Christian.

iv.           iv.  In 303 AD, Diocletian drafted an edict ordering the destruction of Christian churches, sacred books, etc., and prohibiting Christian worship.

v.              v.    Two fires broke out at Dicoletian’s imperial palace in Nicomedia, which he blamed on Christians and punished them for.

vi.               10)       Diocletian abdicated on May 1st, 305 AD at his palace at Nicomedia, ordering Maximian to also resign in fulfillment of an earlier promise. He nominated Constantius Chlorus and Galerius as the new Augusti, with seniority for Constantius.

w.               a.    Galerius made his nephew Maximinus Daia his Caesar.

x. b.    Constantius Chlorus accepted Galerisus’ friend Flavius Valerius Severus as his Caesar.

y. c.    Maximian was not pleased with his forced retirement, scheming to take back the throne at his residence in Lucania.

z. d.    When Diocletian died, Maximinus exiled his widow Prisca and his only child Valeria. Later, Maximinus’ successor Licinius beheaded Prisca and Valeria.
