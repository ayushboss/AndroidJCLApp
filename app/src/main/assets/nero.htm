Nero (AD 54-68)

 

1.         1)      Nero’s first five, good years are called the quinquennium Neronis. He was guided by Burrus and Seneca and restrained by his mother, Agrippina the Younger.

2.         2)      At the end of Claudius’ reign, the Parthian king Vologeses I put his brother Tiridates I on the Armenian throne. Nero sent Domitius Corbulo, a strict disciplinarian, to the East. Corbulo put Tigranes V, a Roman client on the Armenian throne.

a. a.    Later, in 61 BC, Tigranes V of Armenia dumbly attacked Media, a powerful Parthian ally. The Romans tried to help and got their butts kicked. So Corbulo took command of Roman forces and invaded Armenia, forcing them to recognize Roman supremacy. In return, he allowed Tiridates I to ascend the throne provided he came to Rome to personally receive his crown from Nero.

b.         3)      Nero sent Suetonius Paulinus to Britain. After Suetonius Paulinus conquered the island of Mona, the main center of druidisim, the queen of the Iceni and Trinovantes, Boudicca, staged a rebellion. Her husband, the king Prasutagus, had been killed by Roman procurators (working under tax collectors, e.g. Seneca the Younger). The procurators also raped Boudicca’s daughters and flogged her, and robbed her of her land.

c. a.    Boudicca captured Camulodunum and Londinium. She massacred 70,000 Roman citizens in Londinium.

d.                  b.    Suetonius Paulinus defeated Boudicca and Boudicca killed herself.

e. 4)   Nero executed the freedman Callistus and his aunt Domitia Lepida (grandmother of Octavia and Britannicus).

f.   a.    Domitia Lepida had forced Narcissus to commit suicide upon the death of Claudius. She also had contrived to murder Marcus Junius Silanus, provincial governor of Asia. But Nero executed her.

g. 5)   Wanting to break free of his mother Agrippina the Younger, Nero expelled Pallas, a freedman and her biggest ally in the palace, from his court. Agrippina the Younger was pissed off and threatened to support Britannicus as rightful heir (Britannicus was the son of Claudius and Messalina). In response Nero poisoned Britannicus right before Agrippina’s eyes.

h.         6)      Nero wanted to divorce his wife Octavia and marry his mistress Poppaea Sabina, but Agrippina didn’t want this to happen.

i.   a.    Poppaea Sabina had been the wife of Marcus Salvius Otho. In return for allowing his tryst with Poppaea Sabina, Nero gave Otho the governorship of Lusitania (Portugal).

j.    b.    Nero exiled Octavia and married Poppaea Sabina. Nero killed Poppaea Sabina while she was pregnant by kicking her in the stomach, because he was batshit crazy.

k.                  c.    After killing Poppaea Sabina, Nero attempted to marry Antonia, the daughter of Claudius. She was eventually executed.

l.   7)   Nero killed his mother Agrippina the Younger. He tried to with a collapsing boat, but she swam to safety and then he ordered Seneca and Burrus to have the praetorian guard kill her. Anicetus killed Agrippina the Younger, acting on Nero’s orders.

m.      8)      When Burrus died in 62 AD, Nero replaced him with Tigellinus (Gaius Ofonius Tigellinus), the prefect of the vigiles.

n.         9)      Nero was into the arts, performing, music, theatre, etc., and toured Greece. He proclaimed Macedonia free of its governor, in thanks for Greece’s welcoming attitude.

o.         10)    Nero was at Antium during the great fire at Rome in 64 AD. When he came back, he built an extravagant golden palace (the domus aurea). He scapegoated the Christians, a process described by Tacitus (who also hated Christians).

p.         11)    Gaius Calpurnius Piso, whose first marriage Caligula had destroyed, plotted against Nero—the famous Pisonian conspiracy. In the conspiracy were Lucan (Seneca’s nephew) and several Praetorians. Because  of the conspiracy, Seneca, Lucan, and Petronius had to commit suicide.

q.         12)    Nero forced Domitius Corbulo to commit suicide when an alleged plot against the throne was discovered at Beneventum.

r.  13)       Tigellinus’ co-praetorian prefect Faenius Rufus rebelled against Nero at some point.

s.  14)       The Jewish Revolt under Nero

t.   a.    Judea was incensed by the plundering of their temples’ treasures and by Nero’s lack of response when the Greeks attacked their Jewish neighbors in the city of Caesarea.

u.                  b.    The imperial legate of Syria, Cestus Gallus, beseiged Jerusalem but had to retreat when the winter came.

v. c.    Nero gave Titus Flavius Vespasianus (Vespasian) a special command to quell the uprising in Judea. In 67 BC, Vespasian drew a noose around Jerusalem by taking the countryside. Among those who surrendered was Josephus, commander of the rebels in Galilee and future historian..

w.               d.    After Nero: in 70 BC, Titus, Vespasian’s elder son, stormed and captured Jerusalem. A relief on the Arch of Titus shows this destruc tion and a triumphal procession bearing the spoils from the Temple of Jerusalem, including a menorah. The funds from the sack were used to fund the Colosseum.

x. e.    At Masada, a rebel stronghold, all but two women and five children committed suicide when Flavius Silva took Masada.

y.         15)    The revolt of Julius Vindex in Gaul against Nero in 68 AD

z. a.    Nero was prevented from touring Asia Minor and Egypt when the governor of Gallia Lugdunensis, Julius Vindex, rebelled against him. By spring, Otho (governor of Lusitania) and Servius Sulpicius Galba (governor of Hispania Citerior) also supported Vindex against Nero.

aa.             b.    Lucius Verginius Rufus, Nero’s loyal commander in Upper Germany, led three legions into Gaul and defeated Vindex at Vesontio. Vindex committed suicide.

bb.            c.    But Galba went to Rome and bribed the Praetorians, also winning the support of the Senate. Galba was declared emperor. Nero, after saying his last words “qualis artifex pereo”, had a faithful freedman kill him.

cc.              d.    Nymphidius Sabinus, Tigellinus’ successor as the Praetorian Prefect of Nero, urged the Praetorian Guard to defect to Galba.

Galba restored Nero’s fallen statues and took the name Nero.
