Augustus and the Julio-Claudians

 

1.         1)      28 BC—Augustus and Agrippa are co-consuls

2.         2)      27 BC—Augustus makes a big show of giving up powers, and is voted proconsular imperium over the two Spains, Gaul, Egypt and Syria

3.         3)      23 BC—Augustus gets or renews tribunicia potestas.

a. a.    Augustus renews the pre-Republican position of praefectus urbi and makes Messala praefectus urbi but Messala steps down after six days after senatorial opposition.

b.         4)      Augustus’ first choice for succession is Marcellus, his nephew (Marcellus is the son of Augustus’ sister Octavia by her first husband). Augustus has his daughter Julia marry Marcellus.

c. a.    In 23 BC Augustus falls gravely ill and entrusts his signet ring to Agrippa since Marcellus is not old enough to assume the principate. Eventually, Marcellus dies. Augustus has his daughter Julia marry Agrippa (he makes Agrippa divorce his wife).

d.         5)      Following Marcellus’death, Augustus adopts the children of Agrippa and Julia as Gaius Caesar and Julius Caesar. The remaining child of Agrippa and Julia is Agrippa Postumus.

e. 6)   When Agrippa’s heart fails in 12 BC, Gaius and Lucius Caesar are too young, so Augustus turns to Livia’s son by Tiberius Claudius Nero, Tiberius. Tiberius’ popular and well-liked younger brother, Drusus I, dies in Germany in AD 9 by falling off a horse.

f.   7)   Gaius and Lucius Caesar die, Gaius of wounds in Armenia. So in AD 4, Augustus officially adopts Tiberius, and Tiberius is obviously Augustus’ successor.

g. a.    Augustus also adopts Agrippa Postumus, Agrippa and Julia’s surviving child.

h.                  b.    Augustus makes Tiberius adopt his own nephew Germanicus, the son of Drusus I (Tiberius’ brother) and Antonia (daughter of Antony and Octavia).

i.   c.    Augustus makes Germanicus marry Agrippina, a daughter of Agrippa and Julia.

j.    d.    Tiberius’ own son, Drusus II, is relegated to unimportance since he is only a Claudian and not a Julio-Claudian. 

 

Augustan Foreign Policy

 

a. 1)   Augustus personally fights the Cantabrians and Asturians in northwestern Spain, but his health fails and Agrippa defeats the Cantabrians and Asturians through enslavement.

b.         2)      Augustus divides the old Gallia Comata into Lugdunensis, Aquitania, and Belgica.

c. 3)   Augustus forms a veterans’ colony at Auguta Praetoria in the Alpine region of Italy.

d.         4)      Augstus’ stepsons Tiberius and Drusus I subdied Raetia (Switzerlad) and Noricum (Austria and Tyrol)

e. 5)  In Germany: from 12 to 9 BC, Drusus I performed well against the Germans, but died of a broken leg from falling off a horse. His brother Tiberius succeeded him, but Tiberius was called to suppress a rebellion in Pannonia and Illyricum. Tiberius’ successor in Germany, Quinctilius Varus, was creamed along with his three legions by Arminius at Teutoberg Forest in AD 9.

f.   6)  Western Northern Africa: Caesar had annexed Numidia as the province Africa Nova, some of which Augustus gave to King Juba II (who had married Cleopatra Selene).

g. 7)   Marcus Agrippa fought against the Danubians and Pannonians in 13 BC; after his death, Tiberius finished the job from 12 to 9 BC.

h.         8)      Augustus separated Macedonia from Greece by creating the province of Achaea, with its capital at Corinth. Augustus also annexed Cyprus as a separated province from Cilicia.

i.   9)   Client Kingdoms

j.    a.    Following Actium, Augusuts gave large territories in Asia Minor—Galatia, Pisidia, Lycaonia, most of Cilicia—to Amyntas the Galatian. Rome acquired Galatia and Pamphylia when Amyntas was killed.

k.                  b.    Augustus enlarged Judea, kingdom of Herod I, a koing who built the Third Temple at Jerusalem and was a wife and son killer.  A decade after Herod I’s death, Augustus made Judea and Samaria a combined sub-province attached to Syria.

l.   10)       Armenia and Parthia

m.               a.    Armenia was annexed as a province in 34 BC, but had slipped away from Roman control right before Actium. Artaxias gained control of Armenia and slew all Roman citizens. For a while, Augustus did not punish Artaxias.

n.                  b.    Phraates IV was ruling Parthia. Tiridates, a rival of Phraates IV, came to Augustus with Phraates IV’s son, and Augustus gave Tiridates refuge.

i.                 i.     In 23 BC, Phraates IV agreed to give back the legions and prisoners from Carrhae in exchange for his son. 

ii.             ii.   In 20 BC, Artaxias of Armenia was killed and Augustus sent Tiberius with a Roman army to place Tigranes III on the Armenian throne.

1.                  1.    Phraates IV got scared and immediately handed over the standards.

2.                  2.    Augustus declared a great victory with slogans such as signis receptis, civibus et signis militaribus a Parthis recuperatis, and Armenia recepta.

iii.           iii.  When Tigranes III of Armenia died, the Armenians put their own king on the throne. Augustus sent his grandson Gaius Caesar to put a Roman king on the throne. Gaius Caesar died of his wounds from the fighting.

iv.      11)    Egypt and the Read Sea Zone

o.                  a.    Egypt was mostly quiet except for skirmished on the Nubian border in Upper Egypt.

p.                  b.    Gaius Cornelius Gallus was the first prefect of Egypt. He was an accomplished general, elegiac poet, and friend of Augustus, Vergil, and Asinius Pollio.

q.                  c.    Cornelius Gallus led an expedition against the Nubians (sometimes incorrectly called Ethiopians) in 29 BC.

i.                 i.     In later campaigns, Gaius Petronius, a later prefect, repulsed counterattacks from Nubia and drove them back into the Sudan, destroying their holy city of Napata.

ii.             ii.   Candace of Ethiopia, queen of the Nubians, finally agreed to a fixing of the boundary of Egypt about 60 miles south of Syene and the First Cataract.
