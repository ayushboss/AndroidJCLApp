The Rise of Constantine the Great: 306 – 312 AD

 

1.         1)      Constatine the Great was the son of Constantius Chlorus and Helena. Constantius Chlorus later divorced Helena to marry Theodora, Maximian’s stepdaughter (Maximian was then Diocletian’s Caesar in the West).

a. a.    Constantine was sent to Diocletian’s court in the East to get military training. After Diocletian died, the court passed onto Galerius.

b.                  b.    Constantine distinguished himself during Galerius’ victorious Persian campaigns against Narses in 298 AD.

c. 2)   Constantine and his father Constantius Chlorus defeated the Picts in Britain, before Constantius Chlorus died at Eburacum.

d.                  a.    According to Lactantius, a different story: Constantine met his father Constantius Chlorus while he was on his deathbed in Eburacum (York).

e. b.    After Chlorus’ death, the army immediatley proclaimed Constantine an Augustus, but Constantine remained loyal to Diocletian’s Tetrarchy system by allowing Flavius Valerius Severus, Chlorus’ former Caesar, to replace Chlorus as Augustus.

f.   c.    When Flavius Valerius Severus became Augustus, Constantine became Caesar.

g. 3)   Maxentius, the son of Maximian, was mad that Constantine had become Caesar, and started his ururpation in 306 AD. He believed that as son of an ex-Augustus, he had more right to being a Caesar. Maxentius was supported by the Praetorians and the population.

h.                  a.    Flavius Valerius Severus attempted to dislodge Maxentius and his allies. After failing, Flavius Valerius Severus fled to Ravenna, where Maxentius treacherously murdered him. Maxentius now declared himself Augustus.

i.   4)   In 308 AD, Galerius called a meeting with Diocletian and Maximian (Maxentius’ father, who had now turned against Maxentius).

j.    a.    Galerius’ old comrade Licinius was appointed to replace Flavius Valerius Severus, and Maxentius was condemned as a usurper.

k.                  b.    Maximinus Daia and Constantine were not happy that Licinius had hopped over them to become Augustus.

l.   5)   Constantine held the consulship with Galerius in 307 AD, showing his loyalty.

m.      6)      Constantine divorced his first wife, Minervina (mother of his son Crispus) to marry Maximian’s daughter Fausta.

n.                  a.    In 308, Constantine helped Maximian; but in 310 Maximian tried to subvert the loyalty of Constantine’s troops and committed suicide after Constantine captured him.

o.                  b.    By Fausta, Constantine had Constans, Constantius II, and Constantine II.

p.         7)      In 310 AD, Constantine adopted Sol Invictus as his patron deity and claimed he was descended from Claudius Gothicus, in the process repudiating the Herculian Dynasty.

q.         8)      In 311 AD, Galerius (the senior Augustus) issued the Edict of Toleration.

r.  a.    Galerius had become convinced of the futility of persecuting Christians.

s.  b.    The Edict of Toleration allowed Christians all over the Empire freedom of worship and the right to reopen their churches, if they would pray for Galerius and the state and not disturb the public order.

t.   9)   When Galerius died, Maximinus Daia seized Galerius’ asiatic provinces and threatened Licinius’ control of the Balkans.

u.                  a.    Constantine anticipated a war with Maximinus Daia and therefore allied with Licinius.

v. b.    Constantine betrothed his half-sister Constantia to Licinius.

w.               c.    So Constantine was allied with Licinius; meanwhile, Maximinus Daia had made a secret alliance with the usurper Maxentius.

x. 10)       Constantine Invades Italy, 312 AD

y. a.    At Turn, Constantine defeated cavalry sent by Maxentius.

z. b.    In 312 AD, Constantine defeated Maxentius at the Battle of the Milvian Bridge.

i.                 i.     Maxentius had originally intended to defend the walls of the city built by Aurelian, but changed his plans for some reason (omens? public opinion?)

ii.             ii.   As part of his earlier plan, Maxentius had destroyed the Milvian bridge and replaced it with boats in order to hinder Constantine. This did not help him.

iii.          iii.  According to Lactantius, Constantine had a vision that bade him place the Christogram (Chi and Rho) on his soldiers’ shields.

1.                  1.    According to Eusebius, Constantine told him years later that he had seen a flaming cross across the face of the sun with the words “in hoc signo vinces.”

aa.             c.    After the battle of the Milvian Bridge, the seante declare Constantine senior Augustus.

i.                 i.     Constantine entered the city with the head of Maxentius on a stick, and Maxentius’ memory was damned.

bb.            d.    Constantine was getting converted to Christianity but not immediately.

i.                 i.     Constantine’s Triumphal Arch shows Sol Invictus as his patron deity.

ii.             ii.   Constatine ordered Maximinus Daia to discontinue his persecution of Christians in the East. Constantine ordered his proconsul in Africa to restore confiscated property to the churches and to give funds to Caecilianus, the newly elected Bishop of Carthage.

iii.     11)    In 313 AD, Constantine called together the Conference of Milan, where a) Licinius and Constantia got married and b) the two emperorrs reached an agreement on the freedom of religion and the status of Churches. An Edict of Milan was Published.

cc.              a.    Maximinus Daia  hadcontinued to persecute Christians after the Edict of Toleration. He refused to desit in 313 AD when Constantine ordered him to.

dd.   12)    In 313 AD, Maximinus Daia attacked Licinius. Licinius defeated Maximinus Daia at the Battle of Adrianople, but Daia escaped by disguising himself as a slave. In Asia Minor, Maximinus Daia fell ill and died.

ee.    13)    Constantine and Licinius mutually feared each other, leading them to go to war in 316 AD. After a few years of apparent harmony, they went to war against.

ff.                  a.    Constantine declared war on Licinius because he had reneged on the Edict of Milan and had started perseucting Christians again.

i.                 i.     In 324, Constantine went through Licinius’ territory to confront a Gothic invasion of Moesia and Thrace. Constantine rejected Licinius’ protest.

ii.             ii.   In 324, Constantine attacked and defeated Licinius. Constantine spared Licinius’ life due to the pleading of Constantia (Constantine’s half-sister and Licinius’ wife), but he executed Licinius six months later for treason anyway.

iii.     14)    Constantine had allowed the Pope to decide on correct Christian doctrine.

gg.             a.    The Donatist Schism: In Africa, the fanatical Donatus railed against bishops who had yielded under Diocletian’s persecutions. Constantine condemned him and ordred the military suppression of the Donatists and the confiscation of their churches.

hh.            b.    The Arian Heresy: Licinius had started persecuting Christians again due to the disruptive Arian Heresy. Arius was an Egpytian priest who, like Donatus, opposed leniency toward lapsed Christians. Arius also contended that Christ was not of the same substance (homoousious) as God, but of different substance (heteroousios), and was created subsequently and posterior to God.

i.                 i.     Alexander, bishop of Alexandria, excommunicated Arius.

 

Constantine

 

i.   1)   The Council of Nicaea (325 AD): Constnatine gathered 300 bishops to Nicaea in Bithynia for an ecumenical council to restore unity to the Church.

b.                  a.    Constantine had written Arius and Alexander a letter telling them to put aside their squabbles for the sake of the church.

c. b.    At the Council of Nicaea, Constantine made a speech at the beginning.

d.                  c.    The Council formulated the Nicene Creed, later modified minorly at the Council of Constantinople at 381 AD.

e. d.    The Council of Nicaea affirmed the doctrine of the Trinity and excommunicated Arius.

f.   e.    Constantine claimed to be Isapostolos (Equal of the Apostles) and the elected servant of God.

g. 2)   Constantine founded the city of Constantinople on the site of Byzantium and moved the Empire’s capital to Constantinople. In 324, he began fixing up Byzantium. Constantinople was the “New Rome” and pretty much identical to old Rome.

h.                  a.    To beautify Constantinople, Constantine ransacked ancient temples and shriens, even Delphi, from which he took the tripod and statue of Apollo.

i.   b.    Constantine laid the foundations of a new Byzantine Empire through founding Constantinople.

j.    3)   In 326 AD, Constantine executed his son Crispus on charges of raping his stepmother Fausta.

k.                  a.    Empress Fausta probably engineered the rape charge against Crispus to remove a rival to her own sons by Constantine: Constantine II, Constantius II, Constans.

l.   b.    In 326 AD, Empress Fausta died when scalded in a hot bath.

m.               c.    Helena, Constantine’s mother, had revealed that Fausta had committed adultery with a slave.

n.         4)      In 337 AD, Constantine was preparing to lead an army against Persia when he fell sick; he visited the healing springs of Helenopolis but was not curied.

o.                  a.    Constantine asked Eusebius, a bishop who had defended Arius at Nicaea, to administer his sacrament of baptism.

p.                  b.    Constantine was buried in a mausoleum connected with the Church of the Holy Apostles.

 

Constantius II (337 – 361 AD)

 

a. 1)   After Constantine’s death, Constantius II supposedly created false rumors that Constantine’s two half-brothers had murdered Constatine. As a result, troops killed the half-brothers and most of their families: only Constantine’s young nephews Gallus and Julian survived.

b.         2)      Constantine II (the oldest son), Constantius II, and Constans had been Caesars; each recevied the rank of Augustus upon Constantine’s death.

c. a.    Constantine II attacked Constans and was killed.

d.                  b.    Constans was overthrown and executed by Magnentius, a high ranking officer of British and Frankish ancestry.

e. c.    Constantius II had been fighting Shapur II of Persia; disengaging from Persia, Constantius II defeated Magnentius, who committed suicide.

f.   3)   Constantius II chose his cousin Gallus as Caesar and put him in charge of the East.

g. a.    Gallus married Constantius II’s sister, Constantia/Constantina.

h.                  b.    When Constantina died and Gallus was succesful against the Persians, as well as against the powerful interests at Antioch, Constantius recalled Gallus to Italy and executed him.

i.   4)   Constantius II next made Julian, another nephew of Constantine and the twnety-three-year-old half brother of Gallus, his Caesar.

j.    a.    The Empress Eusebia, wife of Constantius II, had urged Julian’s appointment. When Eusebia died in 359, Constantius II became jealous and suspicious of Julian.

k.                  b.    Constantius II demanded several of Julian’s troops for a “Persian threat.” Julian’s troops refused. After peace offers for joint rule had failed, Constantius set out to attack Julian in 361 AD.

l.   5)   Before Constantius II could fight Julian, he became ill and died.

m.      6)      Constantius II was a moderate Arian. He summoned two regional councils of bishops, at Ariminum in the West and Selecuia in Cilicia in the East, in 359 AD.

n.                  a.    In 360 AD, under Constantius II, The Council of Constantinople decalred that the Son was “like the Father.”

 

Julian the Apostate (361 – 363 AD)

 

a. 1)   Julian had been raised a Christian, and educated by Bishop George of Cappadocia.

b.         2)      At Cappadocia, Julian read Greek philosophy and Classical literature, becoming attracted to the spirit of Hellenism (especially since Christians had murdered his family and forced him into exile).

c. 3)   In 361 AD, Julian entered Constantinople and rescinded laws hostile to Paganism, proclaiming religious toleration for all and using his subtle powers to advanced paganism against Christianity.

d.         4)      Julian worked to create a “Neoplatonic syncretism of pagan cults with the sun god as the Universal One” and give this pagan synthesis a unified institutional structure.

e. 5)   Julian practiced magic, divination, omens, theurgy, and elaborate sacrifices, which contributed to Christians seeing him as an agent of Satan.

f.   6)   Julian rescinded privileges previously granted to Christian churches and declared an end to Imperial financial support for Christian churches.

g. 7)   Julian forbade Christians to teach the classic works of Greco-Roman literature and philosophy, intending to marginalize them from the dominant culture.

h.         8)      Julian’s Persian War against Shapur II

i.   a.    Julian thought his divine destiny would protect him, and wanded himself to win glory as another Alexander the Great. Therefore he attacked Persia under Shapur.

j.    b.    Julian’s base was at Antioch, a staunchly Christian city he unsuccessfully tried to paganize.

k.                  c.    Julian had success early on, but failed to capture Ctesiphon.

l.   d.    Julian was killed by a mortal spear wound to his side; according to some, a disgruntled Christian in his own ranks threw it. Shapur II used this to taunt the retreating Romans.

 

Jovian (363 – 364 AD)

 

a. 1)   After Julian died with no heir, his generals chose Salutius Secundus, a Pagan and Praetorian Prefect of the East as well as close adviser to Julian, as their first choice. But he was old and unambitious and therefore refused.

b.         2)      Their second choice was Jovian, a Christian officer.

c. 3)   Jovian concluded a treaty with Shapur II.

d.                  a.    The Romans surrendered Nisibis, a stronghold in Mesopotmaia, abandoned provinces beyond the Tigris, ceded the Roman protectorate over Armenia, and paid an annual subsidy to Persia.

e. 4)   Jovian rescinded Julian’s anti-Christian legilsation.

f.   5)   Jovian died after reigning eight months.
