Nerva (96 to 98 AD)

 

1.         1)      Nerva came from a family of distinguished jurists, but one which had only gained aristocratic acceptance through the mariiage of his maternal uncle with a Julio-Claudian. Nerva was consul in 71 AD under Vespasian, and again in 90 under Domitian.

2.         2)      Nerva vowed never ot put a senator to death without a senatorial trial, restored the senate’s administration of grain, suspended the maiestas (treason law), recalled senatorial exiles, and suppressed informers. As such, the senate loved him.

3.         3)      Nerva was forced to allow the Praetorian Guard to kill Domitian’s assassins, including their former Praetorian prefect.

4.         4)      Nerva adopted Marcus Ulpius Traianus (Trajan) as his son, hier and co-regent. Trajan was the governor of Upper Germany.

 

Trajan (98 to 117 AD)

 

1.         1)      Trajan was the first emperor of provincial origin, and was from Spain.

2.         2)      Trajan was born at Italica near Hispalis (Seville), in the Spanish province of Baetica.

3.         3)      Trjana had a military career under Vespasian and Domitian, and had won the title of Germanicus as governor of Upper Germany under Nerva.

4.         4)      Trajan styled himself an optimus princeps, a title officially assumed in 115 BC.

5.         5)      Trajan greatly increased the number of provincial senators, making provincial appointees account for 40 percent of the senate.

6.         6)      Trajan introduced or greatly expanded the alimenta, a program of public assistance for freeborn children.

7.         7)      Trajan invaded Dacia in 101 AD, fighting against King Decebalus of Dacia.

a. a.    Trajan was defeated by Decebalus at Tapae near the Danube’s Iron Gates.

b.                  b.    In 102 AD, Trajan invaded Dacia again and captured the Dacian capital of Sarmizegethusa, stationing a permanent garrison there. Decebalus surrendered and agreed to become a Roman client. Trajan received the title Dacicus.

c. c.    In 105 AD, Decebalus rebelled again. Trajan defeated him and Decebalus committed suicide, and Trajan imported 50,000 Dacian war prisoners as slaves in the arena.

d.                  d.    After Decebalus’ suicide, Trajan annexed Dacia as a province and made Sarmizegethusa a colony (Ulpia Traiana).

e. 8)   Trajan defeats Parthia and Chosroes, the Parthian King: 113 to 117 AD

f.   a.    Chosroes, the new Parthian king, deposed the king of Armenia without Rome’s consent. Trajan conquered one Parthian capital, Ctesiphon.

g. 9)   Revolts under Trajan: Seleucia, Mesopotamia, Assyria, Armenia revolt.

h.                  a.    The most serious revolts are of the Jews in Cyrenaica, Mesopotamia, Adiabene (northern Assyria), Cyrpus, and Egypt: savage massacres.

i.   b.    Trajan’s Mauretanian general Lusius Quietus the Moor helped him.

j.    c.    Trajan captured and destroyed Seleucia on the Tigris, pacifying southern Mesopotamia.

k.         10)    Trajan Dies in 117 AD: On the road back from Ctesiphon, he became ill. Setting out for Rome, he left Hadrian (Publius Aelius Hadrianus) in command of the near East. Trajan died at Selinus and Cilicia of a stroke.

l.   11)       Summary: By conquering Dacia and Parthia, Trajan expanded the empire to its greatest extent.

 

Hadrian (117 to 138 AD)

 

a. 1)   After the death of Trajan the armies of Syria proclaimed Hadrian emperor, and the senate confirmed him.

b.         2)      Hadrian was borna t Rome in 76 AD, of Spanish parents. His mother was from Gades, while his father was from Italica in Baetica, the same town where Trajan was born.

c. a.    Hadrian was orphaned at 10 and became a ward of Hadrian, his father’s cousin and closest male relative.

d.         3)      Hadrian loved Greek art and philosophy, and received the half-contemptuous nickname Graeculus. Hadrian loved a handsome young Bithynian Greek called Antinous whom he met on an Eastern tour in 123 AD.

e. 4)   Hadrian was Trajan’s quaestor in the First Dacian War. In the Second Dacian War, Hadrian commanded a legion. He was governor of Lower Pannonia and later Syria.

f.   5)   Hadrian married Trajan’s grandniece, Vibia Sabina.

g. 6)   Hadrian’s trusted friend Marcius Turbo replaced Trajan’s moorish chieftain, Lusius Quetus.  Marcius Turbo helped him quell revolts in Cyrenaica, Egypt, Cyprus, Moesia, Danube, in Britain, etc.

h.         7)      Trajan abandoned all recent Roman conquests east of the Euphrates, allowing Greater Armenia to revert to a client kingdom, and making piece with Parthia. Trajan celebrated a magnificent triumph over Parthia.

i.   8)   Lusius Quietus and three other former chieftains were unhappy with Hadrian’s abandoing of expansionist policies. They were executed, supposedly without Hadrian’s knowledge.

j.    a.    Revolt broke out in Mauretania after Lusius Quietus’ execution, and was put down by Marcius Tubo. Hadrian also led an expedition against the Moors.

k.         9)      Hadrian built Hadrian’s Wall in Britain, which stretched from Solway Firth to the Tyne and was designed to keep restrain Northern tribesmen.

l.   a.    Hadrian was the first emperor to built large-scale fixed frontier defenses.

m.      10)    Under Hadrian, Chrosroes of Parthia declared war but decided to negotiate when Hadrian arrived with impressive troop concentrations.

n.         11)    In Athens, Hadrian completed the Olympeion, a huge temple to Olympian Zeus which the tyrant Peisistraus had begun seven centuries before.

o.                  a.    Hadrian built the city of Hadrianopolis in the suburbs of Athens, and he built the Panhelleion (a temple dedicated to Greek unity) in Hadrianopolis.

p.         12)    In Judaea, Hadrian resolved to found a Roman colony called Aelia Capitolina on the site of Jerusalem.

q.                  a.    Hadrian also erected a temple to Jupiter Capitolinus in the Temple of Jerusalem, thereby provoking the rebellion under Simon Bar Kokhba.

r.  b.    The Jews captured Jersualem, slaughtered a Roman legion under Bar Kokhba.

s.  c.    Hadrian appointed Julius Severus to quash the rebellion. Severus slaughtered half a million people and quelled the revolt in 135 AD.

t.   d.    After Hadrian’s general Julius Severus defeated the Jewish Revolt of Simon Bar Kokhba, Hadrian forcibly removed the surviving Jewish Population of Jerusalem.

i.                 i.     Hadrian renamed Jerusalem Aelia Capitolina.

ii.             ii.   Hadrian renamed Judea Syria Palestina.

iii.     13)    Hadrian frequently bestowed the right of Latinum Maius (Greater Latinity) which allowed citizenship for all the decurions, town councils, and magistrates of a town.

iv.      14)    Hadrian continued Vespasian’s policy of using equites as the heads of executive departments rather than Claudius’ policy of using freedmen.

v. 15)       Under Hadrian, the Concilium Principis, formerly an informal conclave of palace advisers and friends, began to become a more formal Imperial Council.

vi.      16)    Hadrian made the unanimous opinions (responsa) of distinguished jurist the force of law and binding upon judges trying similar cases.

vii.   17)    Hadrian commissioned Salvius Julianus to draw up a Praetorian Perpetual Edict, binding on all present and future praetors unless authorized by the emperor or senate.

viii.        18)       In accordance with Stoic philosophy, Hadrian made it illegal for a master to kill, torture or castrate slaves, to sell them as gladiators, or to use them for lwed or immoral purposes. Hadrian deprived the paterfamilias of the power of death over his children.

ix.      19)    Hadrian spent his last years at his beautiful villa at Tibur (Tivoli), up the Anio.

x. 20)       Hadrian’s first choice to succeed him was Lucius Ceionius Commudus, whom he adopted as Lucius Aelius Caesar. When Lucius died, Hadrian adopted Antoninus Pius (Titus Aurelius Antoninus), a rich and virtuous senator.

u.                  a.    Hadrian required Antoninus Pius to adopt Lucius Ceionius Commodus, the seven year old son of Aelius Caesar, and Marcus Aurelius (Marcus Annius Verus), the seventeen-year-old nephew of Antoninus Pius’ own wife.

v. b.    Marcus Aurelius was betrothed to Aelius Caesar’s daughter, but broke it off so he could marry Faustina, Antoninus Pius’ daughter.

w.      21)    Hadrian died in 138 AD at Baiae near Naples. Antoninus Pius buried his body in a special mausoleum (the Mausoleum of Hadrian, now the Castel Sant’ Angelo) and had Hadrian deified against the wishes of the Senate.

 

Antoninus Pius (138 to 161)

 

a. 1)   Titus Aurelius Antoninus won the title of Pius for burying Hadrian, getting him deified.

b.                  a.    Antoninus Pius prevented the senate from annulling the acta of Hadrian, in return abolishing the four hated judgeships of Italy and sparing proscribed senators.

c. 2)   Antoninus Pius was born and raised at Lanuvium, though his rich aristocratic family came from Nemausus (Nimes) in Southern Gaul.

d.                  a.    Antoninus Pius had an estate at Lorium which pleased him a lot.

e. 3)   Hadrian made Antoninus Pius district judge of Italy in 120 and proconsul of Asia in 135. Later Hadrian made Antoninus Pius a member of the consilium principis and his successor.

f.   4)   Antoninus Pius had an intimate knowledge of the law, and five jurists were on the Imperial Council. He increased penalties against masters who killed their slaves.

g. 5)   Antoninus Pius built a wall in Scotland between the firths of Forth and Clyde. Antoninus Pius’ Wall was built of Turf and Clay and was 37 miles. Antoninus pushed the border of the frontier about 75 miles to the North.

h.         6)      Antoninus Pius had a peaceful career and died at Lorium in 168 AD. In his delirium he talked of the lying kings tand the Empire that had betrayed him.

 

Marcus Aurelius (161 to 180)

 

a. 1)   Marcus Aurelius was a devotee of Stoic Philosophy and wrote the Meditations in Greek while encamped along the Danube during the Marcommanic Wars.

b.                  a.    Aurelius studied Stoicism under Junius Rusticus, who lent him Epictetus’ Discourses.

c. 2)   Fronto was Marcus Aurelius’ tutor. Fronto taught him rhetoric and law. Marcus Aurelius corresponded with Fronto for many years.  Lather Aurelius was taught by Maecianus and Atticus, law and rhetoric respectively.

d.         3)      Marcus Aurelius faced many disasters: a flood of the Tiber, a famine in Galatia, a revolt in Britain, a German crossing of the Rhine, and an invasion of Armenia and Parthia by the young Parthian king Vologeses III.

e. 4)   Marcus first act on emperor was to appoint his adoptive brother, Lucius Ceionius Commudus the Younger, as his colleague with the new name Lucius Verus. Marcus Aurelius’ daughter Lucilla married Verus later. Verus went to deal with Parthia.

f.   5)   Lucius Verus fights Vologeses III of Parthia: 161 to 165 AD

g. a.    Verus’ subordinates were Statius Priscus and Avidius Cassius.

h.                  b.    Statius Priscus invaded Armenia and captured and burned Artaxata, its capital.

i.   c.    Avidius Cassius, a Syrian-born taskmaster, crossed the Euphrates and invaded Mesopotamia, capturing Edess, Nisibis, Ctesiphon and Seleucia. Avidius Cassius burnt Seleucia to the ground. Soldiers returning from Seleucia brought plague.

j.    d.    Verus died of a stroke while returning from Danube preparations due to the plague.

k.         6)      The Marcommanic War along the Danube: 168 to 175

l.   a.    Aurelius fought against the Quadi, Marcommani and Iazyges (of Sarmatia), as well as the Chatti and Chauci.

m.               b.    In 170, the Quadi and Marcommani defeated Aurelius and swept into Italy, besieging Aquileia, the seaport at the head of the Adriatic.

n.                  c.    Aurelius granted temporary peace with the Quadi and Marcommani in order to deal with the Moors, who had plundered the Temple of Mysteries at Eleusis.

o.                  d.    Aurelius found pretexts to attack them when he had dealt with the Moors. This set off the Second Marcommanic War.

p.                  e.    Marcus Aurelius has a column in the Campus Martius which commemorates the  Marcommanic Wars.

q.         7)      Avidius Cassius rebels against Marcus Aurelius, 175 AD

r.  a.    Avidius Cassius was governor of Syria, and he rebelled when misled by the false rumor of Marcus Aurelius’ death.  Before Aurelius could defeat him, a legionary showed him with the head of Cassius, which Aurelius ordered buried reverently.

s.  8)   The Third Marcommanic War: 178 to 180 AD

t.   a.    Aurelius crushed the Quadi and Marcommani, and built a legionary camp at Castra Regina on the Danube. But, he did not conquer their towns.

u.                  b.    After the Third Marcommanic War, Aurelius cretaed two new provinces, Marcommania and Sarmatia.

v. 9)   Aurelius made his successor, his son Commodus, co-emperor in 177 AD.

 

Interlude I: Roman Architecture in the First Two Centuries AD

 

a. 1)   Imperial Palaces

b.                  a.    Emperors all had palaces on the Palatine hill.

c. b.    Nero’s Domus Aurea. Vespasian destroyed the Domus Aurea and replaced it with a smaller palace.

d.                  c.    Hadrian was an innovative architect and designed his own villa near Tibur.

e. 2)   Public Buildings

f.   a.    The Flavian emperors destroyed the Domus Aurea and built the Colosseum and baths of Titus on its site.

g. b.    Titus began the Arch of Titus, at the eastern entrance to the Forum. It commemorates his victory against the Jews, and has a menorah.

h.                  c.    Vespasian began the Temple of Peace (also called the Forum of Vespasian) to commemorate the end of the Jewish revolt. The Forum of Vespasian was southeast of the Forum of Augustus and just North of the original forum.

i.                 i.     Vespasian also completed the temple of the Deified Claudius.

ii.             ii.   Vespasian probably built the Templum Sacrae Urbis (Temple of the Sacred City)

i.   d.    Vespasain began and Domitian finished the Colosseum, originally called the Flavian Amphiteatre, which received the name Colossus from the massive statue of Nero near it, which Vespasian reworked into a statue of the sun god.

j.    e.   

k.         3)      The Forum of Trajan is the largest Imperial forum and the Forum of Nerva is the smallest.

l.   4)   Hadrian’s Public Buildings

m.               a.    Hadrian built tht Temple of Venus and Rome, the largest temple in Ancient Rome, between the Colosseum and the Temple of Peace (Forum of Vespasian).

n.                  b.    Hadrian built the Pantheon in Rome, now a Christian Church. The original Pantheon was built by Agrippa, Augustus’ colleague. An oculus (a round opening in the top) is at the apex of the cylindrical ceiling.

o.                  c.    Antoninus Pius buried Hadrian in Hadrian’s tomb, a colossal round mausoleum across the Tiber, now the Castel Sant’ Angelo.

p.         5)      Trajan’s Column in the courtyard behind the Basilica Ulpia commemorated the Dacian Wars. The narrative on the column culminates with the death of King Decebalus of Dacia.

q.                  a.    Marcus Aurelius imitated Trajan’s column with his own in the Campus Martius.

 

Commodus (180 to 192 BC)

 

a. 1)   Commodus was proclaimed a Caesar in 166 AD, at the age of five. At fifteen, he was made consul, granted imperium, and given the title of Augustus. From fifteen, he was a joint ruler with his father.

b.         2)      Commodus made several congiaria (donations of money) and set up a trade route from Africa to Rome to curry popular favor.

c. a.    Commodus used the javelin and bow to kill wild beasts in the arena. He trained and fought left-handed as a secutor. He boasted of 620 victories in an inscription.

d.                  b.    Commodus had his head placed atop the Colossus and added the club and lion skin of Hercules to it. The Colossus had been dedicated to Nero and then to Helios.

e. c.    Commodus dressed in lion skin, carried a club, and associated himself with Hercules. He printed coins with the inscription HERC COMMODUS. He later associated himself with Jupiter, Mithras, Sol, Cybele, Serapis, and Isis.He also embroidered his clothes with gold.

f.  d.    Commodus renamed Rome the colony of Commodiana.

g. 3)   Commodus’ sister Lucilla was married first to Lucius Verus and then to Titus Claudius Pompeianus, who advised Commodus to finish the Marcommanic War. Commodus negotiated instead, abandoing Aurelius’ plans for a frontier on the Elbe River and the Carpathian Mountains.

h.                  a.    Commodus himself was married to Bruttia Crispina, who didn’t produce an heir.

i.   4)   Commodus’ first palace chamberlain (cubicularius) was Saoterus, a Bithynian Greek freedman.

j.    5)   In 182 AD, Lucilla and senators plotted against Commodus. Claudius Pompeianus, Lucilla’s husband, was not involved,

k.                  a.    Lucilla’s daughter by Lucius Verus and the daughter’s two lovers, Quintianus (Claudius Pompeianus’ relation) and Ummidius Quadratus, were involved.

l.   b.    Quintianus bungled the assasination of Commodus by proclaiming that the senate was sending the dagger in his hand.

m.               c.    All the conspirators were executed and arrested, including Lucilla, after a brief exile to Capri. Commodus also suspected and execute his wife, Bruttia Crispina. Claudius Pompeianus retired from public life to avoid suspicion.

n.         6)      Two members of Ummidius Quadratus’ household later became close to Commodus: the freedman Electus became Commodus’ chamberlain and the freedwoman Marcia became Commodus’ concubine.

o.         7)      Publius Tarrutienus Paternus and Sextus Tigidius Perennis, Commdous’ two joint Pretorian Prefects, brought about the murder of his chamberlain Saoterus.

p.                  a.    Paternus was promoted to the Senate and Perennis became the sole Pretorian Prefect. Perennis then engineered Paternus’ execution on charges of planning to put his son-in-law on the throne.

q.                  b.    Perennis removed senatorial generals from their commands and replaced them with equestrian legates. He removed two who later became emperor: Helvius Pertinax (protégé of Claudius Pompeianus and governor of Syria) and Septimius Severus.

r.  c.    Cleander later recalled Pertinax as well.

s.  d.    Didius Julianus later became emperor too; he was a relative of Paternus’ son-in-law.

t.   8)   The so-called Deserters’ War, a series of provincial mutinies, occurred under Commodus.

u.         9)      Under Perennis, there was trouble in Northern Britain. A new governor retrived the situation, allowing Commodus to be proclaimed Britannicus. But then people rebelled against the governor; Perennis fell before he could address this.

v. 10)       In 185 AD, a group of palace freemen led by the chamberlain Cleander and including the freedwoman Marcia overthrew Perennis. Cleander and Marcia appointed Imperial administrators in exchange for bribes.

w.               a.    Cleander took the unprecedented step of appointing himself Praetorian Prefect.

x. 11)       In 187 AD, Cleander was broughtd own when the curator of the grain supply (praefectus annonae) created a grain shortage that the public blamed on Cleander. Helvius Pertinax, Pompeianus’ protégé, may have had a hand in it.

y. a.    Quintus Aemilius Laetus replaced Cleander as Praetorian Prefect. Laetus was from Africa, the province when Pertinax had recently been proconsul.

z. b.    Laetus appointed Septimius Severus as governor of Pannonia, a Danubian province.

i.                 i.     Septimius Severus had been Pertinax’s legate in Syria.

ii.             ii.   Septimius Severus’ brother was apponted governor of Lower Moesia.

aa.             c.    Clodius Albinus, another African provincial, was appointed commander in Britain.

bb.   12)    Before his assasination, Commodus was planning to move permanently into the galadiatorial barracks next to the Colosseum and kill the two new consuls on January 1st, proclaiming himself sole consul in their place.

cc.     13)    Commodus’ Praetorian Prefect Laetus, his freedman Electus (who had replaced Cleander), and his concubine Marcia plotted against him.

dd.            a.    Marcia tried to poison his food, but Commodus vomitted it due to drinking.

ee.             b.    Commodus’ wrestling partner, Narcissus, and another freedman then strangled him as he was bathing. He died on December 31st, 193 AD
